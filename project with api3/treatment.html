Before the invention of CT (Computed Tomography) scans, diagnosing brain tumors was extremely challenging. Physicians relied on physical examinations, neurological assessments, and patient-reported symptoms such as headaches, seizures, or vision problems. Imaging options were limited to X-rays and angiography, which often failed to detect small or deep-seated tumors. This meant that many brain tumors were discovered only at advanced stages, making treatment more difficult and less effective.

The introduction of CT and MRI scans revolutionized the diagnosis of brain tumors. These imaging techniques allow doctors to view detailed cross-sectional images of the brain, identify tumor size, location, and characteristics quickly and accurately. The process is non-invasive, fast, and widely available, making early detection easier and more reliable. Patients can now receive precise diagnoses without the invasive procedures required in the past.

Once a brain tumor is diagnosed, several treatment options are available depending on the type, size, and location of the tumor:

  • Surgery: Removal of the tumor is often the first approach if it is accessible and safe to operate on.
  • Radiation Therapy: Uses high-energy rays to target and kill cancer cells, often used after surgery.
  • Chemotherapy: Medication that targets tumor cells, sometimes in combination with radiation therapy.
  • Targeted Therapy: Drugs that specifically target tumor cell mechanisms while sparing normal cells.
  • Immunotherapy: Boosts the immune system to fight tumor cells.
  • Supportive Care: Symptom management, such as controlling seizures, headaches, and swelling.

Modern treatment plans often combine several of these options to maximize effectiveness. Early diagnosis via CT or MRI allows doctors to create a personalized and effective treatment plan, significantly improving patient outcomes.
